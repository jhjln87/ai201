$$\int_{1}^{2} \left(3x^3 - \frac{1}{x^2}\right) dx$$

### Step 2: Substitution and Simplification

No complex inner functions or $u$-substitution are required for this problem. The integrand can be integrated directly by rewriting the fraction with a negative exponent:


$$3x^3 - \frac{1}{x^2} = 3x^3 - x^{-2}$$

### Step 3: Algebraic Transformation & Technique Selection

We will use the linearity property of integration to split the terms and apply the standard Power Rule for integration, $\int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$.

### Step 4: Analytical Evaluation (Applying Integration Rules)

Find the antiderivative $F(x)$ by integrating each term individually:


$$\int 3x^3 \, dx = 3 \cdot \frac{x^4}{4} = \frac{3}{4}x^4$$

$$\int -x^{-2} \, dx = -\left(\frac{x^{-1}}{-1}\right) = \frac{1}{x}$$

Combining these gives the general antiderivative:


$$F(x) = \frac{3}{4}x^4 + \frac{1}{x}$$

### Step 5: Boundary Evaluation & Finalization

Now, we apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, evaluating $F(x)$ from $1$ to $2$:


$$\left[ \frac{3}{4}x^4 + \frac{1}{x} \right]_{1}^{2} = F(2) - F(1)$$

* **Evaluate at the upper bound ($x = 2$):**

$$F(2) = \frac{3}{4}(2)^4 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4}(16) + \frac{1}{2} = 12 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{25}{2}$$


* **Evaluate at the lower bound ($x = 1$):**

$$F(1) = \frac{3}{4}(1)^4 + \frac{1}{1} = \frac{3}{4} + 1 = \frac{7}{4}$$


* **Subtract $F(2) - F(1)$:**

$$\frac{25}{2} - \frac{7}{4} = \frac{50}{4} - \frac{7}{4} = \frac{43}{4}$$
